When you start to learn Amharic, you’ll very quickly realize that the writing system bears little resemblance to anything else you’ve seen.Unlike European languages, Amharic does not technically use an alphabet, it uses what’s known as an abugida - or alphasyllabary - known as Ge’ez (Ge’ez is to Amharic as Latin is to English, it uses the same script, but today Ge’ez is used only as a liturgical language of the Ethiopian Orthodox Church). We recommend learning the alphaber side-to-side with the lesson.

When it comes to nouns, Amharic usually has derived or primary nouns. It means that a noun could be primary like “leg” in Amharic (ǝgǝr) while others are simply derived forms such as “pedestrian”, (ǝgr-äñña). Like in most languages, gender in Amharic is either feminine or masculine and expressed in diverse ways. A number of adjectives and nouns end up with a marker that signifies femininity, such as “bäg” for ram and “bäg-it” for ewe. In essence, femininity in the language doesn’t just help to distinguish the sex of the subject but helpful when indicating the smallness of a subject, compassion or tenderness.

Learning Amharic can have many benefits, including:
  • Improved communication with the native speakers of Amharic, which is the official language of Ethiopia.
  • Greater cultural understanding of Ethiopia and the Horn of Africa, which has a rich history and diverse society.
  • Enhanced career opportunities, as knowledge of Amharic can be valuable for business or government work in Ethiopia.
  • Increased cognitive benefits, as learning a new language can improve cognitive function, memory, and problem-solving skills.
  • Personal growth and self-fulfillment, as learning a new language can be a rewarding and challenging experience that can deepen one's understanding of the world.
